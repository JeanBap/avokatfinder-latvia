Dalita Ipasums (Divided/Shared Property Ownership)

Dalita ipasums covers various forms of co-ownership and shared property arrangements under Latvian law, including fractional ownership and ownership of apartments in multi-unit buildings where the land is separately owned.

Latvian property law recognises kopipasums (co-ownership), where two or more persons own undivided shares in a single property. Each co-owner's share (domajama dala) is registered in the zemesgramata. Major decisions — selling or mortgaging the whole property — require all co-owners' consent. Routine management decisions can be made by majority vote.

A distinctive Latvian situation arises in apartment buildings where the apartments are individually owned but the land beneath the building belongs to a different owner (private landowner, municipality, or state). This 'separated ownership' (dalita ipasums) means apartment owners pay a ground rent (zemes nomas maksa) to the landowner. This creates ongoing costs and can complicate sales and mortgages.

The zemes nomas maksa is typically a percentage of the land's cadastral value and is paid annually. Its existence is recorded in the zemesgramata. Prospective buyers of apartments in dalita ipasums buildings must understand that they will pay an indefinite ground rent in addition to their normal ownership costs.

Latvia has been gradually resolving the dalita ipasums issue through compulsory buyout legislation, giving apartment owners the right to compulsorily purchase the land beneath their building at a judicially determined price. This process is ongoing and some buildings remain in the separated ownership situation.

The kompetenta zemes sagatavosana (land consolidation) programme has also been used in some areas to reunite separated land and building ownership. Buyers should check the current status of the land title before purchasing an apartment in a multi-unit building.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a Latvian apartment without checking whether the building land is separately owned means you may be signing up for indefinite ground rent payments that significantly increase your total housing cost.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: For any apartment purchase, ask your lawyer specifically: 'Who owns the land under this building, and is there a zemes nomas maksa?' If yes, find out the annual amount and the status of any compulsory buyout process.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the zemes nomas maksa?

The ground rent paid by the apartment building's occupants (or management entity) to the private or state landowner who owns the plot beneath the building. It is an ongoing cost not reflected in the purchase price.

Can the compulsory buyout of separated land be forced by one apartment owner?

No. The compulsory buyout must typically be initiated collectively by the apartment building's owners. Individual owners cannot force a buyout unilaterally in most circumstances.

Is separated land ownership being abolished in Latvia?

The Latvian government has enacted legislation to phase out separated ownership arrangements, giving apartment owners the right to compulsorily acquire the land at a court-determined price. The process is still ongoing for many buildings.
